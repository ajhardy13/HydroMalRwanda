HydroMal: A hydrology-based spatial intelligence system for national scale vector control planning

Introduction

This document is to provide guidance for the use of the Google Earth Engine tool HydroMal that enables users to visualise and explore a number of information layers relating to hydrology, that might help with designing national-level vector control strategies.

These information layers are derived from freely available terrain data (the shape of the landscape) and outputs from the TropWet tool that uses freely available satellite imagery to estimate the fraction of water and vegetation on the ground at a resolution of 30 m. For more information on the TropWet tool please see: here.

Notes and Tips

DEM: Digital Elevation Model. This represents the elevation of the landscape. Dark areas are relatively low lying (low elevation) and light areas are relatively high (high elevation). On its own it can be used for context but carries no direct hydrological information.

Back to top 

Hillshade: Again, for context only, this layer casts a shadow over a DEM giving an impression of change in terrain across the landscape.

Back to top 

TWI: Topographic Wetness Index. This layer is calculated using a DEM. Specifically, this takes into account how flat or steep an area is, and how large the upstream area is. For instance, flat slopes at valley bottoms tend to be wet because a) flat slopes retain water better than steep slopes and b) valley bottoms have a large upstream area with lots of water flowing in from the surrounding slopes. Therefore, TWI can be seen as a measure of the likeliness of an area to be wet or dry. High TWI = higher chance of wetness (red colour), low TWI = lower chance of wetness (blue colour). High TWI values are typically associated with swampy areas or river channels. Where an area is completely flat no TWI values will be generated, but this typically represents large lake features.

Back to top 

HAND: Height Above Nearest Drainage Network. The TWI is used to define the drainage network, i.e. the likely path of streams and rivers, based on areas where the TWI value is highest (i.e. wettest). HAND represents the relative height above that drainage network and can be a good indicator of flooding. The lower the HAND value (blue colour), the more likely the area is to be inundated with water. Conversely, high HAND values are less likely to be inundated with water (red). Here, anything above 15 m is removed under the assumption that it will never be inundated with water.

Back to top 

Slope: Calculated using a DEM, this layer represents the angle of the area. High values correspond to steep slopes (white), low values correspond to flat areas (black).

Back to top 

Water, Vegetation, Bare Earth Fraction Layers: This set of layers is generated using the TropWet tool using the Landsat satellite image archive. For each 30 x 30 m pixel in the image an estimate of the fraction of water, vegetation and bare earth is made. This is displayed as a red, green, blue image.

For instance, shades of red demonstrate the presence of water. Where a pixel is completely red, this is likely to be completely covered in water. Where a pixel has mixed shades of red and green (possibly an orange colour) this suggests that the pixel is mixed with water and vegetation. These differences could be important in identifying water sources that represent mosquito breeding sites, with many anopheles species preferring slow moving or stagnant water bodies with emergent or aquatic vegetation.

These layers are calculated for the approximate dry season (for Rwanda June-August) and wet season (for Rwanda March-May) for each year between 2010-2020. As such, users will find 20 different layers to choose from.

Back to top 

Water, Vegetation and Water Permanence Layers: This set of layers is similar to the above in that it is developed using the TropWet tool with the red colour again representing fraction of water and green representing fraction of vegetation per 30 x 30 m pixel. Again, this is calculated using dry season and wet season images for each year between 2010-2020.

But with these layers, blue represents the permanence or persistence of water. High values mean that a fraction of water has been observed in that area often over the entire 2010-2020 period. So, for example, an area that appears magenta is covered in water on that timestep and has remained wet for most of the 2010-2020 period. An area that is blue is wet for that particular time step but is likely to be only temporarily wet as it has rarely been observed to be wet before.

Above Average Wet Season Layers: These layers are calculated in the same way as the Water, Vegetation, Bare Earth Fraction Layers but this represents the wet season during periods that are considered to be wetter than average. A year is identified as being wetter than average based on the NASA GRACE-derived Water Equivalent Thickness dataset over Rwanda demonstrating broadscale soil water content since 2002.

Back to top 

Above Average Dry Season Layers: Same as above but for the dry season.

Back to top 

Generate RGB Composite: Here, users have the ability to generate their own composite image showing three different layers at once, colour coded red, green and blue.

For instance, you may be interested in areas with a high TWI value, that were inundated with water in the wet season of 2020 and are permanent water bodies. We would add these to the composite builder, noting that band 3 of the Water, Vegetation and Water Permanence layers represents water body permanence.

Notes and Tips

Cloud: The data layers generated from TropWet use “optical” satellite data. This means that cloud-free conditions are needed at some point over a three month period. Cloud free images are stitched together to give a seamless image. However, some periods are so cloudy that there aren’t any cloud free images to use. When this happens you will see gaps in the results like the example below:

Forest: Similar to the issues with cloud, the satellite imagery used by TropWet cannot see through tree canopies. Therefore, we need to be careful when interpreting the results over forested areas. To help users, we have provided a data layer showing where forests exist. You can view this layer using the button in the bottom-left of the interface i.e. “Show Forest Mask”. In the example below you can see a large area of forest in western Rwanda (indicated by the dark green colour). The TropWet tool may wrongly identify areas within this region as being a mix of water and vegetation.
